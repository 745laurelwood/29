[Players and Cards]
29 is played by four players in fixed partnerships, partners facing each other.

32 cards from a standard 52-card pack are used for play. There are eight cards in each suit: ranked from high to low as: J-9-A-10-K-Q-8-7. The other cards from 6 down to 2 are not used. The aim of the game is to win tricks containing valuable cards. The values of the cards are:
- Jacks: 3 points each
- Nines: 2 points each
- Aces: 1 point each
- Tens: 1 point each
- K, Q, 8, 7: 0 points each.
Ace is bigger than 10 for the purposes of a trick, as defined by the order above.

This gives a total of 28 points for cards. The last trick is worth an extra card point, for a total of 29, which explains the name of the game.

[Deal and Bidding]
Deal and play are clockwise; the cards are shuffled by the dealer. Four cards are then dealt to each player, one at a time.

[Bidding phase]
Based on these four cards, players bid for the right to choose trumps. The player next to the dealer (first one to get cards) goes first. Each bid is a number, and the highest bidder undertakes that their side will win at least that many points. Subsequent players, in clockwise order, may either bid higher or pass. The minimum bid allowed is 16 and the maximum is 29. If a subsequent player bids higher, the previous player who had the highest bid just before this is given the right to match this bid, or raise their bid even higher. A player can also choose to pass and not bid, in which case the decision moves to the next player. Once a player passes on bidding, they can never bid in this round again. The auction continues until there's a winner. If everyone chooses to pass, by default the last player (dealer) is assigned a bid of 16 points and gets to choose the trump suit.

The final winner of the bidding phase chooses a trump suit secretly. The dealer then completes the deal, giving four more cards to each player, so that everyone has eight.

[The Play]
The player who sets the trump gets to play first. They choose any card to start the paly, and subsequent players must follow suit if possible. The player with the highest card of the starting suit wins that trick. The winner of each trick leads the next trick. Initially the trump suit is unknown to the players other than the bidder. A player who is unable to follow suit may choose to ask the bidder to declare the trump suit (if not already declared previously); the bidder then reveals the secretly chosen trump suit. In case that trump has been declared already previously, and a player is not able to follow suit, they may choose to play a card of the trump suit. Starting from the trick during which the trump suit is declared, each trick is won by the highest trump in it, or by the highest card of the starting suit if the trick does not contains trumps.

[Royals]
A special rule called royals enables changing the bid mid-game. If a player has both King and Queen of trump suit in their hand at the time when the trump suit is revealed, they show everyone the pair (royals) and either of these two things happen:
1. If the player with the royals belongs to the team who won the bidding phase, their team's bid is decreased by 4 points. e.g. if they bid 20, they now only have to make 16 points to win the round.
2. If the player with the royals does not belong to the team who won the bidding phase, the bid of the opponent team (bid winners) increases by 4. So if the bid was 16 points, now the bidders need to make 20 points to win the game.
These changes in bids are still to remain within bounds [16, 29]

Note that a player who originally held the King and Queen of trumps cannot declare them if one or both of them has already been played, and the trump is revealed afterwards.

[Scoring]
When all eight tricks have been played, each side counts the card points in the tricks it has won, the winners of the last trick adding an extra card point. If the bidding side took at least as many card points as they bid, adjusted for a declaration of royals if appropriate, they win one game point; otherwise they lose one game point. The score of the team playing against the bidder does not change.


[Seventh card]
A special rule exists, where if a player has won the bid but is unable to decide which suit to pick as trump in the bidding phase, they may choose to call for the 'seventh card' of their hand to be the trump. In this case, the dealer deals the remaining cards (8 total to everyone) and the 7th card's suit is set as the trump suit. In addition, this 7th card is shown once to the bid winner and then hidden from everyone, only to be revealed when someone asks to declare the trump suit.
